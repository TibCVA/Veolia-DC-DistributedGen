Decentralised Power in Data-Centres – The Case for Action

Real-world grid constraints and carbon scrutiny already compel blue-chip operators to install on-site multi-hour generation.

US-VA (Loudoun Co.)
County Board rejected a 600 MW campus in Mar-24 because sub-station capacity was exhausted; a 150 MW redesign was later accepted.
NL – national
Government imposed "stop-sale zones" and vetoed Meta Zeewolde 120 MW campus (2022) on energy-land conflict grounds.
Singapore – island grid
Moratorium on new DCs 2019-22; lift limited to 300 MW under strict PUE & emissions caps.
Compass Meridian (MS, US)
500 MW campus (Jan-25) will deploy full N+1 diesel farm because utility cannot supply bulk power before 2028.

Operator verbatims

Grid lead-time is the critical path; we spec diesel day-one or we miss launch.
– Hyperscale VP Infrastructure, Feb-25 (interview #3)
HVO lets us keep the same engines and hit CO₂ targets instantly.
– EU Colo CTO, Jan-25 (interview #2)

Causality chain

Connection delay → schedule risk – queues ≥ 18 mo push owners to self-generation.
Licence-to-operate pressure – public opposition (Zeewolde, Loudoun) can downsize or kill projects; lower-carbon backup is a negotiating chip.
Technology response – Tier-4 diesel is fastest, densest, bankable; switching to HVO (renewable hydrotreated vegetable oil) decarbonises without redesign.
Take-away: Decentralised generation has shifted from contingency to default programme item for Tier III/IV projects in power-constrained hubs.
Glossary:
Tier IV = Uptime classification guaranteeing 2 N+1 redundancy; TEC = Transmission Entry Capacity; HVO = drop-in renewable diesel with up to 90 % CO₂e reduction tank-to-wheel.
